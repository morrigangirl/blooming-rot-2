# Phase Two — The Dead Man's Receipts

*A runnable chapter for Blooming Rot, Part 2. Hand it to a GM; it contains everything needed to run the phase at the table.*

> *"Haskur Vandrell did not flee to safety. He fled to an account someone had already opened for him."*

---

## Before you run this

The party went east of the Jotuns and broke a man named Haskur Vandrell — broke him badly, left him for finished — and then watched him walk away from a fight he had no business surviving. They came home to Loftwick to a hero's welcome they did not entirely trust, a quiet old spymaster named Caelith Dunivar who lodged them in the Little Palace and asked them to look at what Haskur left behind, and, at the very end of the evening, a single letter delivered by ordinary post, sealed with a crane in flight above three coins, that read: *We hope your visit to Loftwick is brief.*

This chapter is the morning after that letter. It is not a dungeon and it is barely a fight. It is an **investigation that takes place almost entirely indoors and on foot, across three days in a single city**, and its horror is administrative: forged paperwork, a quietly murdered clerk, a missing grain factor, a polite man with a bribe, and a professional thief sent for evidence rather than blood. By the end of it the party will understand that the man they beat was never the point — that he was a line item in somebody's ledger, pulled back from death twice in nine years because killing him was cheaper to undo than to allow — and they will leave Loftwick riding hard for the east, because every road they pull on runs the same way.

Run it warmly and run it slow. The pleasure of this chapter is watching intelligent players assemble a conspiracy out of receipts.

### What is actually happening (for your eyes only)

Hold this in your head; the players earn it a piece at a time, and some of it they never get.

A Rel Astran banking house — **Cindren & Vhal, Bonded Factors**, with a grand counting house in Rel Astra, a working branch in Hardby, and a single sleepy notary's office here in Loftwick — has spent at least four years laundering paperwork for a client whose name never appears. One of the firm's services to that client is a standing contract on Haskur Vandrell: whenever he falls, a pre-laid contingency yanks him to a safe house and the firm re-files him under a new name. They have done it twice before. The party's fight was the third.

To keep that machine quiet, the firm has been spending money in Loftwick. They paid a Treasury bonds clerk named **Merro Gilst** for four years to look the other way — and the moment Haskur's escrow was drawn down and Gilst became a man who could notice, they had a contract physician named **Reln Pollow** certify a tidy heart-failure on a man who had in fact drunk a spiked cup of tea. And a grain factor named **Tamsin Moraven**, who had been pulling at the firm's false harvest-receipts, walked out of her counting house eleven days ago and did not come back. She is alive. She is being held in Hardby. They have not yet decided what to do with her.

Above Cindren & Vhal sits someone the firm answers to, who signs only with the initial **"E."** Beneath all of it lies a craft older and stranger than anything the Yeomanry can name. **The party must not learn either of those things in this chapter.** They will brush against both — a countersignature here, a lacquered tube whose workmanship no sage can place — and they must leave with the questions open. Those are the campaign's long fuses; do not light them early. (There's a short list of exactly what never to say at the table at the back of this chapter under *Things you never name.*)

What the party *can* prove is the firm, the dead clerk, the missing woman, and the single hard fact that ties them together: someone at Cindren & Vhal's **Hardby** branch personally underwrote Haskur Vandrell six weeks before the party ever met him. The hand is in Hardby. That is where the road goes next.

### The shape of the chapter

Three movements, across roughly three in-fiction days and two to four sessions.

The first is **the table.** The party comes down to breakfast and finds Haskur's possessions laid out as evidence in daylight; Caelith tells them who he really is and what he wants; and the morning's reading turns a handful of objects into three names and two streets to walk to.

The second is **the city.** The party works two neighborhoods at their own pace. **Wick Lane** is the dense one — the firm's local office, the dead clerk's rooms, the physician's surgery, and a watcher posted on the corner who turns the street into a footchase. **Tannerway** is the human one — the missing woman's counting house, her husband's house, the watch sergeant who failed her. Whichever thread they pull, it points east; the quiet triumph of the chapter is the moment they lay the threads side by side and see the road for themselves.

The third is **the squeeze and the road.** Loftwick stops being a safe place to work. A genial Council deputy arrives with a bribe and a warning; a thief comes for the evidence in the night; and Caelith lays three ways east on the table and asks the party to choose how they want to travel — fast and blind, slow and protected, or quiet and deniable. They ride out at dawn. Hardby is six days off. Rel Astra is further, and closer than it looks.

### The five truths

You can let almost anything in this chapter go sideways except these. The party should leave Loftwick believing, in their bones:

1. **Haskur survived by design, not luck.** Someone paid, in advance, to pull him back from death.
2. **He is a node in a network, not a lone agent** — a name the system moves around like an account.
3. **Cindren & Vhal sit at the center, and the living hand is in Hardby.**
4. **Loftwick will not let them finish this here.** There is pressure, political and criminal, to make them stop.
5. **The road runs Loftwick → Hardby → Rel Astra, and it must be taken now, with urgency.**

Every one of these is carried by *at least three* different clues in the chapter, so no single missed roll or skipped room can cost the party the truth. If you reach the final scene and one of the five hasn't landed, the *Quick reference* at the back tells you which late line supplies it. You should arrive at the gate with all five in hand and the party arguing about *how* to go east, not *whether*.

### The heartbeat — the Pressure Clock

This chapter runs on a clock, and you should put it on the table where everyone can see it: six segments on a notecard, filled in one at a time. It is not a doom timer. It is the sound of the conspiracy noticing the party — and every time it ticks, *something happens in the fiction* that hands the party a new thing to react to. A clerk reports that the evidence was disturbed in the night. A note arrives warning their host to throw them out. The street starts gossiping. A witness goes cold.

You advance it when the party does something the conspiracy can *see from outside the building* — showing their hand at the firm's office, rattling the physician, making a public spectacle — and when a whole day passes with nothing accomplished. You do **not** advance it for honest investigation done quietly; re-reading the evidence at the breakfast table costs nothing.

Aim to reach the final scene with the clock at three or four. That's pressure landing on the party without ever having stopped them. The full segment-by-segment table, and what to do if the clock runs too fast or too slow, are at the back under *The Pressure Clock in full* — but you can run the whole chapter knowing only this: **when in doubt, let the clock tick, and read what it does out loud.**

# Movement One — The Table

## Scene 1 · The Morning Review

*The Refectory of the Little Palace, the second bell after dawn. The morning after the crane-and-coins letter.*

Overnight, Caelith brought Haskur's possessions up out of the cellar strongroom and laid them along the great oak table — not as treasure, but as evidence, each object on its own square of numbered linen. When the party comes down to breakfast, this is what they find, and a stranger already sitting with the morning's tea.

::: read
Morning comes gray over Loftwick. The Little Palace smells of beeswax, hearth smoke, boiled coffee, and sealed wax. Outside, the city has already begun celebrating a victory it does not understand — bells somewhere beyond the garden wall, hooves over street stones, a clerk in the hall arguing softly with a militia sergeant about who is allowed in.

In the Refectory the brass chandeliers are low-lit, the lacquered paneling drinking what little light comes through the leaded windows. Caelith Dunivar has laid the dead man's possessions across the long table. Each object rests on a square of linen. Each square is numbered in his precise hand. The room feels less like a sitting chamber now and more like a battlefield after all the blood has learned to write.

A woman is already at the table, a cup of pale tea balanced on one knee. Her dress is the color of a forest at dusk; a small silver leaf rests at her collar. She does not stand when you enter. She inclines her head a careful inch and sets the cup down.

Caelith looks up. "I have good news," he says. "The man you fought was almost certainly Haskur Vandrell." He taps one gloved finger beside a ruined length of dark wood. "The bad news is that Haskur Vandrell has been dead before."

The woman speaks before he can go on, in a voice you have to lean toward. "Good morning. I am Trina. I advise Caelith on people. I am sorry about the envelope at breakfast. I expect we will get to it last."
:::

Let the party settle and take the room in. Trina's introduction is deliberately light; she will not steer the morning unless asked. (If the party's warlock, **Alicia**, is among them, Trina quietly offers her a cup of tea and nothing else — the first thread of a relationship that runs through the whole chapter. See *The Mentor* later on.)

### Caelith tells them who he is

Once they've found chairs, Caelith says his piece. Read it in his voice — measured, every sentence weighed as though it's going on a record — and don't rush the four turns of it. He sets a small flat brass token on the table as he begins: a quill etched above a closed gate.

::: read
"Last night I told you what was true and not for public ears. This morning I will tell you what is true and not yet, by any formal record, anyone's. I prefer that we agree on a standard of trust before we keep working.

"I am, by quiet appointment of the Duke, the Hand. The Yeomanry maintains no formal title for it — you will not find it in any document our Council reads. My office at the Audit Hall is the body of the work. The Hand is the part that goes outside our borders. You have been corresponding with that part since you walked through the Lesser Gate.

"Haskur Vandrell is not a man I learned about last week. He has been on the back of my desk for nine years, and in that time he has died twice — pulled back, like he was pulled back from you, by a contingency someone else laid down ahead of him. Once at sea off Onnwal, in 575; he went into the water between deck and boat and we wrote him drowned. Once on a Hardby tavern floor in 580, bleeding out, until a barmaid I had never identified set a hand on his chest and he was simply not there. When I went to find the barmaid, she was also not there.

"To underwrite a man twice in nine years and keep him moving between cities and pretexts costs more than a guild has, and more than a noble cousin has. It costs the kind of money you only see when an institution is being used like a wallet. That is why your fight matters. He is not the man who made him. He is the man someone keeps making.

"Last night you saw a letter of credit drawn six weeks before your fight, in Rel Astra, endorsed to him or his agent. This morning, in better light, we have seen a great deal more. I would like to walk you through it — and I would like you to tell me what you see that I have not. If, after that, you still wish to take the eastern commission, I will show you three ways to travel and the cost of each. If you wish to leave us to it, I will pay you in full and you will hear nothing further from me. I say it again, in front of Trina, so that no one mishears."
:::

::: gm
Caelith leaves the brass token on the table for the rest of the morning and pockets it only when the party leaves the room; a character watching closely (passive Perception 14) understands he's *showing* it to them, not flashing it. He will answer factual questions and refuse speculation. Two questions usually come up — keep his answers exactly this firm:

- *"Why us?"* — "Because you have already done the part of this work that requires being a stranger to the Yeomanry, and the part I cannot trust to my own clerks is the part where the strangers do the writing."
- *"Who do you suspect in your own office?"* — "I will not name a clerk to you whom I cannot prove. If I named one, and you treated them as proven, I would have made you the wrong kind of Hand."

He is right to worry about a leak, and the chapter will keep brushing against it — but he never names a name, and neither do you. Trina says nothing during the speech. If Caelith glances at her at the end, she nods once. She is the witness.
:::

### The seven things on the table

Now the heart of the morning: the party reads the evidence by daylight. Let them pick objects up and pass them around. Each one rewards a look, and most reward a *closer* look; weave the checks below into the conversation rather than calling for a row of rolls. Caelith offers a single dry line over each as the party gets to it — those lines are the framing you want them to carry out of the room.

**1 · The burned spell focus.** A palm-sized length of dark wood carved with concentric grooves, intact but used hard: one face carries a long scorch down the inside of a groove, with a bead of melted beeswax fused into the char. A character versed in the Art (Arcana DC 13) recognizes that a focus only scorches like this when a *contingency-class* spell has been channeled *through* it — used as the conduit of a pre-laid escape, not as an ordinary casting tool. A closer reading (Arcana DC 16) finds a sigil pressed into the wax-bead that matches nothing in Haskur's own notes: a stylized three-rayed sun closing into a fist. Whoever laid the contingency, it was not him.

> Caelith, quietly: *"This was not instinct. This was a contract being honored."*

::: gm
The sun-into-fist sigil is the mark of the conspirators' patron school of magic. **Do not name it.** A character who pushes for an identification gets only: *"a school I have seen on two unrelated documents in nine years, and never once with a name attached."* That is the whole reveal here. It is a Phase 3-and-later thread.
:::

**2 · The false letter of passage.** From Haskur's wallet of correspondence, a Yeomanry letter of safe passage naming the bearer **Haskur Voren, Agricultural Assessor, in service to the Loftwick Bureau of Crop Estimation** — real vellum, real wax, a chop that matches (Investigation DC 12). But the signature, checked against the Audit Hall's files (Investigation DC 15), belongs to the *previous* Bureau Chief, who retired in 581 — and the letter is dated last summer. Anyone who knows the Bureau's habits (History DC 14) knows it never sends assessors out on itinerant routes at all; they sit and audit by mail. A man carrying this through inns and toll-gates would never be questioned by a watchman — and never dare show his face at a real Bureau audit. Whoever forged it knew exactly that gap. That is an inside job.

> Caelith, dry as winter: *"Agricultural assessor. Very funny, or very grave. We will know which when we learn who pays for the harvest he was counting."*

**3 · The clipped coin.** A heavy silver coin, not Yeomanry mint, its edges shaved thin — and on the clipped edge, a tiny countermark punched into the metal: a tower above three waves. A merchant's eye (Investigation DC 12) reads it as a private exchange mark — someone has "weighed this coin in" against an internal account. A scholar of the eastern ports (History DC 15) places it on the Rel Astran coast; and a very good one (History DC 17) names the house outright: **Cindren & Vhal, Bonded Factors** — the same firm whose crane-and-coins seal countersigned last night's letter of credit. This isn't money. It's a token of account.

> Caelith, holding it to the light: *"This is not a coin. It is a receipt pretending to be money."*

**4 · The black-lacquered tube.** Nested inside Haskur's brass map-case is a second, smaller cylinder — a finger-length tube of black lacquer, sealed at one end with unbroken red wax. It's a courier's vessel: shockproof, watertight, made to ride hidden inside a larger tube (Investigation DC 10). The seal carries a minor abjuration (Arcana DC 13): break it without the passphrase and it discharges a single *command* — the word is **"Forget"** — at whoever opened it (Wisdom save DC 13, single use). And the craft itself, the seal-glyph and the lacquer laid down in seven thin coats where convention asks for four, reads to any sage as elven work — but in a tradition *no one present can place* (Arcana or History DC 17). Inside, once opened: a strip of vellum and a fragment of ledger paper (items 5 and 6). The interior has been wiped clean (Investigation DC 14), but a faint heat-darkened smudge remains — consistent with heat-reactive ink (Arcana DC 15), a message meant to be warmed, read once, and lost. The tube wasn't just a container. It was a relay.

> Caelith, snapping it shut between two fingers: *"The container was bait. Whatever was inside before the seal was last set, we will not know without the man who set it."*

::: gm
This is the single most important thing on the table that the party must **not** resolve. The work is not "elven, but peculiar" because a sage is being modest — it is genuinely beyond living memory, and the most accurate sentence anyone in this world can offer is exactly *"elven, but peculiar."* Under **any** roll, that is the answer. If a player casts *legend lore* or similar, give them a long fragmentary verse about a people who "walked downward and were forgotten," which the caster cannot place — and nothing more. The word you are protecting does not get spoken at this table for a long time yet. (See *Things you never name* at the back.)
:::

**5 · The vellum strip.** Inside the tube, a finger-width strip of fine vellum. Four words in clear capitals: **HARBOR BEFORE THRONE.** No date, no signature. The phrasing is older than current idiom (History DC 13); a character with the right learning (Religion DC 15) places it in two surviving fragments of the *Aerdi Concordat of the Bay*, a fourth-century imperial settlement law holding that a port-city's harbor council must be seated *before* any newly-named throne can collect its revenues — the harbor outranks the crown until the crown acknowledges the harbor. Read as code rather than law (Investigation DC 14), it is an instruction about *timing*: seat the harbor before any new throne can claim its dues.

> Caelith, half-smiling without amusement: *"Power flows through ports before crowns notice. That is older than the Yeomanry, older than Rel Astra. It is also a slogan — which is what people write down when they intend to do something the law will not yet name."*

::: gm
Do not resolve *what* throne or *which* harbor. The party should leave the room with the phrase memorized and uneasy. It pays off in Rel Astra, two chapters from now.
:::

**6 · The ledger fragment.** Also from inside the tube: a torn corner of double-entry paper, no header, no date, three lines in three different hands —

> VANDRELL settled
> MORAVEN delayed
> GILST transferred

Three clerks wrote those lines (Investigation DC 13); this is not one person's notes but a *status board*, kept somewhere a small group passes through. "Settled," "delayed," and "transferred" are accounting verbs (Investigation DC 14): cross-referenced against last night's letter of credit, *VANDRELL settled* matches the very date Haskur's escrow was drawn down — whoever wrote it had live access to the firm's settlement records. And there is not a single sum anywhere on the paper (Investigation DC 14). A real ledger carries figures on every line. This one doesn't, because the money was never the point.

> Caelith, after a long silence: *"There are no sums. That means the money was not the point. People were. They were tracking who was where — and who was no longer."*

This is the fragment that drives the rest of the chapter. **Three names. Vandrell, Moraven, Gilst.** Make sure the table leaves the morning holding them.

**7 · The hidden seed pouch.** Last, Caelith cuts open the lining of Haskur's spellbook in front of the party — he found a small cloth pouch sewn into the back cover overnight. Inside: a handful of wheat seeds and a folded scrap reading *"Seed for the dry barony."* A countryman's eye (Nature DC 13) sees the grain is durum, but blighted sterile at the husk — display seeds, not viable stock; and that sterile durum (Nature DC 16) is exactly what's used to *evidence a harvest that does not exist* to an inspector. "The dry barony" is no Yeomanry estate (Investigation DC 14) — it's an old colloquial name for a rainfall-poor, grain-importing region of the South Province of the Great Kingdom: a perfect customer for receipts on grain nobody grew. And a careful look (Nature DC 17, or with herbalist's tools) finds the husks have been chemically washed to fool a quick eye and fail a slow one. The pouch is a forgery, and it ties Haskur to a false-crop scheme.

> Caelith, turning the treated husks in his fingers: *"Either disguise — or truth wearing a mask. I cannot decide which is more frightening."*

### Where this leads

The party has spent a morning turning objects into questions, and the ledger fragment has handed them the answer to *where to look next.* Push gently off the table and toward the wall:

::: read
Caelith sets the torn ledger-corner back on its square of linen and looks at the three names a moment longer. "Three names, three hands, no sums," he says. "That is not a banker's ledger. It is a roster. And I keep the rolls that turn names into men — who they were, where they posted, where they died. They are downstairs, behind the library wall." He stands, and takes up a candle. "Come. Let us go and make these three into people."
:::

Lead straight into Scene 2. Trina comes with them.

## Scene 2 · Behind the Library Wall

*The Records Alcove, off the Little Palace library. Late morning.*

This is the pivot of the whole chapter, and it does one job supremely well: it turns three bare names into **two streets the party can walk to.** Run it unhurried; this is the moment the investigation stops being Caelith's and becomes theirs.

::: read
Behind the writing desk at the library window, a paneled section of the rear wall swings on a quiet hinge — Caelith works it without comment — and opens on a small warm room you did not see last night. A single oil lamp. Two walls of low shelving, packed with bound civic records the public library does not carry: the *Yeomanry Bonded Firms Charter Roll*; the *Loftwick Treasury Bonds Clerk Register*; *Aerdi Commercial Marks & Counter-Marks*, Rel Astran edition; the *Grain Factor Annual Returns*. Trina takes the chair at the little reading table, draws the first volume someone hands her into her lap, and licks a finger to turn a page. "Names," she says, mildly. "Let us see whose."
:::

Let the party drive the search; Trina assists anyone who asks (her steady hand grants advantage where it helps). Three readings resolve, and each one comes with an address attached — that's the part that matters.

**The firm.** The crane-and-coins seal and the tower-over-waves countermark both resolve, in the marks volume, to **Cindren & Vhal, Bonded Factors** (Investigation DC 12, and DC 15 to tie the internal clearing-mark to the same house). The Charter Roll (History DC 14) shows their shape: senior partners and the real vault in **Rel Astra**; a working branch in **Hardby**; and here in Loftwick only a single notary's office — a satellite, not a branch, unable to hold escrow, staffed by one bonded clerk named **Hesren Vesh**. The address is **Wick Lane.**

**The dead clerk.** *Gilst* resolves in the Bonds Clerk Register (Investigation DC 11) to **Merro Gilst**, a junior Treasury bonds clerk, fifty-one years old, fifteen years in post — his last entry stamped three days before the party rode into Loftwick, and marked *posting closed.* In this register (Investigation DC 14) that phrase means death, retirement, or transfer; there's no retirement and no transfer order on file, so it means he died — yet the certifying physician, one **Reln Pollow**, is not a Yeomanry coroner but a contract physician with rooms on **Wick Lane.** Caelith goes still at that, and asks the party not to call on Pollow until he's had a chance to pull the man's licensure (Persuasion DC 13 to get him to say why: he wasn't informed of the death through his own channels, and that frightens him more than he'll show).

**The missing woman.** *Moraven* resolves in the Grain Factor Returns (Investigation DC 12) to **Tamsin Moraven**, a senior grain factor, twelve years in trade, her counting house on **Tannerway** by the eastern caravan yard — last return filed on time four months ago, nothing since. Cross-referenced with the watch register (Investigation DC 15) there's a missing-person report against her name, eleven days old, taken by a Sergeant **Halir Anwic** and marked *open, unescalated*: she left her counting house with a bag and a cloak, told her staff she was riding to a customer's farm, never named the farm, never came back. And her own filings (History DC 13) are full of pointed queries about South Province grain shipments — exactly the dry-barony pattern the seed pouch points at. She was the Yeomanry's most persistent questioner of false-crop receipts, and now she's gone.

If the party asks Trina about the names, she answers plainly and briefly, and one of them costs her something:

> *Of Cindren & Vhal:* "Twice they came to me for 'small loans,' and twice I said no, and the second time the man who asked stood a little too long in my doorway after he had been thanked. That is not how a clean firm leaves a house."
>
> *Of Gilst:* a single shake of the head. "I did not know him. I am sorry."
>
> *Of Tamsin Moraven* — she pales, but her voice stays level: "Tamsin paid half her daughter's tuition out of her own purse. I paid the other half. Tamsin does not vanish on her own initiative. Find her. Please."

### Where this leads

By the time the lamp's burned down, the party has two neighborhoods and six reasons to walk to them. Make the handoff theirs, not Caelith's — he stays behind on purpose:

::: read
Caelith closes the marks volume and squares it on the shelf. "Wick Lane gives you three doors worth knocking — the firm's office, the dead man's rooms, the physician who signed him away. Tannerway gives you three more — the woman's counting house, her husband's house, the sergeant who took her file and let it cool." He pauses at the panel. "I will not come. My face is known on Wick Lane; yours is not, yet. Go as the strangers you are, and come back and tell me what the city would not say to me."
:::

::: gm
**If the dice are unkind,** don't let the addresses hang on a failed roll. Trina supplies any name the search won't give up — she knew Moraven, she remembers the firm's "odor" — and Caelith's own reading of the registers backstops the rest. No one leaves this room without both streets. From here the party chooses where to start; that choice is the chapter's first real motion, so let them make it. Most parties feel the pull of the dead clerk first — Wick Lane is where the chapter comes alive, and it's where someone is already watching the street.
:::

# Movement Two — The City

The party now works two neighborhoods. Let them go in whatever order they like, and let them spend a *day* in halves rather than splitting the party across the city (if they insist on splitting, call them back together for a working lunch at the Little Palace — see *If they wander* at the back). Whatever they pull on, it runs east. The job of this movement is to let them feel that for themselves.

## Scene 3 · Wick Lane

*A narrow commercial street near the Civic Way. Stationers, scriveners, a notary, a contract physician — the paper trades.*

Wick Lane is where ink gets sold and small fortunes get witnessed. Three of the party's six doors are here, within sight of one another: the firm's green-liveried notary office; the stationer's shop with the dead clerk's rooms above it; and the physician's surgery two doors down. (Dryly, Caelith rents rooms three doors along himself — he mentions the coincidence and plainly does not enjoy it.) It is also, from the moment the party arrives, a street where someone is watching to see who comes asking about Merro Gilst.

::: read
Wick Lane runs barely two carts wide, shaded even at midday by the overhang of second-storey rooms. Apprentices cry quills and sealing-wax from open shutters; a scrivener works a copy-press in a doorway; somewhere a cat is loudly unhappy. Halfway down, a stationer's shop sits under a faded blue sign, and above it two shuttered windows on the second floor — the dead man's rooms, you'll learn. Across the way, a clean dark-green door with a small brass plate: a crane in flight above three coins. And on the corner, hands in pockets, a thin young figure who is not buying anything, and who looks at you a half-second too long before looking away.
:::

Don't make the watcher a puzzle yet — plant the half-second of eye contact and move on. The chase pays it off (Scene 3d). First, the doors.

### 3a · The firm's door — Hesren Vesh

Behind the green door is the Loftwick satellite of Cindren & Vhal: a single front room, a railing, a charter of the firm's marks pinned to the wall, and one bonded clerk in dark-green livery who is courteous to the exact degree that keeps a conversation from happening.

::: read
The man who rises to meet you is perhaps thirty, neat to the point of stiffness, a crane-and-coins pin at his collar. "Hesren Vesh," he says, "for the house of Cindren and Vhal. You'll forgive me — we are a small office. I notarize, I witness, I forward. Anything of substance is Hardby's, or Rel Astra's." He says it the way a man recites the one safe sentence he's been given.
:::

Vesh will confirm the firm's mark from the public charter on his wall and describe, blandly, what the house officially does. He *cannot* open any client account without written authority from Rel Astra, and he'll say so politely, twice. The scene turns on one move: showing him the marginal endorsement from Haskur's letter of credit.

::: gm
When the party puts the endorsement in front of him, his eyes settle on it two breaths longer than they should (Insight DC 14 catches it — he is disturbed, and hiding it). Pressed hard (Persuasion DC 18 or Intimidation DC 17) he gives up exactly four words and nothing more: **"reads like Hardby work."** He will not name a partner; he will not let the party take notes. If they lean harder still, he excuses himself "for refreshments," and uses the minute to send a sealed message out the back by a runner.

Vesh is **not** a conspirator — he's a junior clerk whose seniors are doing something he isn't cleared to know — so don't let the party break him; there's nothing inside to break. But this scene has teeth, because it does two things. It hands the party their first hard east-pointer (*Hardby*). And the moment they leave — or the moment that runner slips out the back — **the watcher on the corner has something to report.** That is one of the two triggers for the chase (Scene 3d). Tipping the firm puts a runner on the street. *(Advance the Pressure Clock one segment: the firm now knows someone is asking.)*
:::

### 3b · The dead man's rooms

Above the stationer's, two rooms a tidy man lived a small life in, sealed by the watch after his death — and already searched, badly, by someone else. The landlord is **Doman Reith**, a careful, anxious man in his sixties who has rented to clerks for thirty years and never had one die on him before.

Getting in is easy if the party has Caelith's written request (he provides one readily, "for private inquiry, not evidence transfer"); Reith reads it twice, asks whether there will be trouble, and unlocks the door. What he says next matters:

::: read
Reith stops with his hand on the latch. "A man came up here yesterday," he says. "Had a watch tabard. I didn't know him — but you don't argue with the watch, do you. He was up here the better part of an hour. Left without a word about what he'd found. I haven't opened it since." He looks at the floor. "I should have asked for his name. I keep thinking that."
:::

Reith isn't lying (Insight DC 13). And his "watchman" wasn't one: the tabard was Yeomanry pattern but the wrong ribbon at the cuff (Investigation DC 12), the kind a man *buys* from a former courier rather than is issued. Whoever searched these rooms was a professional in costume — and didn't find what the party can.

::: gm
The searcher was **Merev Sarth**, the thief who comes for the evidence later in the chapter (Scene 6), on a reconnaissance pass. She didn't have time, and didn't know exactly what she was hunting. The party can succeed where she failed. Let them feel that: the room has been *gone through* — drawers ajar, books shelved a hair out of true, a carpet-corner turned back and not laid flat — and Reith confirms it didn't look like this when Gilst was alive.
:::

The room has been searched, but the searcher missed everything that mattered. There are seven things to find; each rewards a deliberate look. The prize is the second one.

**A · The hollowed book.** On the shelf, the third volume of the *Yeomanry Constitutional Commentaries* sits a finger proud of its neighbors, as if replaced in a hurry — and Gilst was not a man who left books out of true (Investigation DC 13). It's hollow. Inside is a slim receipts book in his own hand: four years of small cash sums from a payer recorded only as **"H.V. Hardby,"** forty-one entries adding to about twelve hundred gold, always cash, always irregular, each small enough to pass for a clerk's bit of private bookkeeping. Three of the entries (Investigation DC 14) are countersigned in a different hand, with a single capital — **"E."** — and those three are larger and oddly timed. Gilst's notes don't say what the countersignature meant; only that on those days the payments were bigger and he wrote no reply.

::: gm
"H.V. Hardby" is the Hardby branch of the firm — Gilst likely thought he was on some junior partner's private retainer and never knew he was being paid by Cindren & Vhal proper. The **"E."** is the party's first brush with the paymaster *above* the firm. **Leave it open.** Caelith doesn't know the initial; Trina doesn't know it; no Yeomanry register lists a relevant figure who signs simply "E." The party leaves with a letter and a question, and that is correct. (See *Things you never name*.)
:::

**B · The smoking gun.** On the writing desk, a small decorative tin of throat lozenges — real lozenges in the top, and under a false floor you can feel the seam of (Perception DC 14, then Investigation DC 12), a single signed bank instrument: made out *to bearer*, for two hundred gold, undated, drawn on **Cindren & Vhal Hardby** and stamped with the very same Hardby chop as Haskur's letter of credit. Same hand. Same chop. This is the document that turns suspicion into proof — it puts Gilst on the firm's private payroll and ties his paymaster directly to the hand that underwrote Haskur. When the party brings it to Caelith, give him a moment to not speak:

::: read
Caelith holds the slip at arm's length, then closer. He sets it on the desk. He looks at Trina, and Trina sets down her tea. "That is a Hardby chop," he says, very evenly. "That is *the* Hardby chop. The man who put it on this paper is the man who put it on Vandrell's escrow. We have the hand now. We do not have a face — but we have the hand." He does not pick it up again. After a while: "You may have just shortened our work by a season."
:::

**C · The locket.** Behind a framed sketch in the bedchamber (Investigation DC 14), a silver locket on a fine chain. Inside, a tiny painted portrait of a dark-haired woman of perhaps thirty, half-smiling. No name, no date. Gilst was unmarried; she's in no register. Leave it a quiet grief — the dead man had a life no one else knew about. (A soft hook for later chapters; nothing to resolve here.)

**D · The burned note.** In the cold hearth, a surviving corner of paper (Investigation DC 13), four words in an unfamiliar hand: *"Do not copy eastern marks twice."* An instruction — Gilst was being told that any copy he made of an eastern clearing-chop was to exist *once*, on the real instrument, never as a duplicate in his own files. He wasn't just paid. He was *managed.*

**E · The coin shaving.** Along the seam of the writing desk (Perception DC 15), a single curl of silver no bigger than a fingernail — a coin shaving (Investigation DC 12), the debris of clipping a coin to private weight, exactly as the countermarked coin on Caelith's table was clipped. Gilst was clipping for the firm too: one more small service, one more small thread they could pull if he ever balked.

**F · The initials list.** Tucked in the receipts book's back cover (Investigation DC 13), a separate small list in a different hand: five sets of initials, each followed by a little thumb-press mark — and one of them, again, simply **"E."** The other four are intermediaries Gilst never met but whose paydays he knew. The party leaves with a list of letters and no faces. *(The "E." is the same "E." — and stays unnamed.)*

**G · The fear note.** Behind the locket's portrait (Perception DC 12), a folded slip in Gilst's own hand, undated:

> *"If they ask again, I do not know how to keep saying no.*
> *If they stop asking, I do not know what that will mean.*
> *This is the third winter I have been afraid."*

Read in sequence with the rest, the room tells a story: a man paid, then pressured, then asked to do something he tried to refuse, then quietly removed. Coercion, resistance, death.

> Caelith, holding the slip: *"He was using us before he was using them. He just ran out of time to tell us so."*

**Before they go,** Reith asks two things: that they re-seal the door with their own wax, and that they keep his name out of anything that reaches the watch. He's right to be afraid. (Caelith later sends him a quiet annual stipend through an unnamed channel; Reith never quite works out where it comes from.)

### 3c · The physician two doors down — Reln Pollow

If the party calls on **Reln Pollow** — the contract physician who certified Gilst's death — they meet a soft, anxious man in his mid-forties with ink-stained fingers and a poorly kept beard, who is far more frightened than a routine three-day-old certification should make anyone.

::: read
Pollow's surgery smells of camphor and old paper. He confirms it readily enough — yes, he certified the Gilst man, heart failure, nothing remarkable; the poor fellow was alone in his chair, half a cup of tea gone cold at his elbow. He says it twice, the way people do when they're listening to how it sounds. His hands do not quite stop moving.
:::

He's too nervous (Insight DC 14) — and a closer read (Insight DC 17) tells you the nerves aren't about the death three days ago; they're about something he expects to happen *now.* (He's right. The conspirators will dispose of him too if the chapter heats up.) Pressed hard (Persuasion DC 18 or Intimidation DC 16) he sweats and gives up nothing actionable, only: *"I did the work I was asked to do. I'm sorry the man is dead. He was kind to me. He gave me a peach last summer."*

::: gm
**This is a rescue, not an interrogation.** Pollow killed Gilst, but he's the disposable instrument, not the ring above. If the party threatens to pull his license, he begs **two days** to leave Loftwick. *Granting it is the right move* — Caelith will hold him to it through Hardby contacts and keep a live witness in play. If the party refuses, Pollow is poisoned the next evening and a witness is lost — but he leaves one trembling line behind, naming his paymaster only as **"the man at the Brass Crow."** (That's a seed for a much later chapter in Greyhawk; if Pollow dies, make sure the party gets the note.) Either way the scene points the party onward. *(Confronting Pollow advances the Pressure Clock one segment — leaning on him is visible from outside.)*
:::

### Where 3a–3c lead

By the time the party has worked the doors of Wick Lane they should be holding three east-pointers from one street: the **Hardby chop** on Gilst's bank instrument, Vesh's **"Hardby work,"** and — once the chase pays out — **"east on the Sevenday cart."** Which brings us to the watcher on the corner.

## Scene 3d · The Sparrow's Run *(the chase)*

This is the chapter's one true footrace: an investigation on Wick Lane flushes a lookout the party can chase but **cannot catch** — and the chase pays them a clue whether or not they ever lay a hand on her. Run it loud and fast and let the table breathe after two movements of quiet paperwork.

::: gm
**How to think about this scene before you run it.** The watcher is *meant* to get away, and she gets away cleanly because she is escaping across her own ground — she used to run messages for a living and she knows the lanes the way the party never will. You can say that out loud; it isn't a cheat, it's her job. The one rule that makes the scene fair: **the clue is not the capture.** She sheds it partway through the run, so the party recovers it on a win, a loss, or a draw. Catching her is a *bonus* — and even then she knows almost nothing, so the design never breaks. Put a little **Lead** tracker on the table (start it at **2**); the party drives it down by gaining ground, the watcher drives it up; she escapes when the route runs out. Keep it to three or four rounds and never let one bad roll end it.
:::

### Flushing her

The chase begins when the party's work on Wick Lane gives the watcher something worth running to report — most naturally, when they lean on Vesh or come back out of Gilst's sealed rooms carrying something. Give them the catch first:

- A character keeping half an eye on the street (passive Perception 14, or an active Perception/Insight DC 13) notices the thin young figure from the corner has drifted to keep a clean sightline on the stationer's door, and *moves when the party moves.*
- If no one clocks her, she peels off to report the moment the party leaves Gilst's rooms — and someone may catch the movement then (DC 12).
- And if all else fails, Reith hands her over unprompted: *"There's a young one's been hung about the corner since the watch-coat man came. Coyle's girl, I think. Hungry-looking."*

The instant the party moves to take her, she runs.

::: read
She doesn't startle — she *launches*, already three strides gone before the nearest of you has shifted weight, ducking under a scrivener's elbow and away down Wick Lane toward the throat of the Cloth Market, with the ease of someone who has run this exact street with the watch behind her. A scrap of oilcloth flaps at her shoulder. She does not look back.
:::

### Who she is

**Anla "Sparrow" Coyle**, about sixteen — wiry, wary, a former message-runner thrown out of work when the little courier-house she ran for folded. A fortnight ago a man she never got a clear look at left coppers and a chalk-stub at a dead-letter niche with a job: *watch the stationer's door, mark who goes up to the dead clerk's rooms and who calls on the green-coats, leave a tally every Sevenday, keep nothing.* She doesn't know who pays her. She doesn't know what it's for. She knows the lanes of Loftwick better than the watch does, because she used to outrun the watch for a living. She is, deliberately, a frightened kid — which is the conscience this scene is built around.

> **Sparrow, in brief.** AC 12 · HP 9 · Speed 35 ft. · Acrobatics, Stealth, and streetwise all sharp. *Nimble Bolt:* she Dashes or Disengages as a bonus action every round — the engine of her escape. She carries a fleece-hook and the oilcloth fold, and no weapon she will use on anyone.

### The run, beat by beat

Four stretches of Loftwick, each a quick obstacle the party can beat in more than one way. Call for one check per pursuer per stretch; ground gained drops the Lead, ground lost raises it. (Rename the streets to fit your Loftwick if you like — these are placeholders.)

**1 · Down Wick Lane into the Cloth Market.** She breaks down the length of Wick Lane and spills into the lunchtime crush of the cloth-sellers' square at its foot. The crowd is the obstacle: a pursuer can shove straight through (Athletics), vault a stall (Acrobatics), bellow the crowd aside (Intimidation, or Persuasion — *"Make way!"*), or read her line and cut the corner (Insight or Investigation). **DC 13.** She uses the press and her Nimble Bolt to open the gap.

**2 · The market wall and the Loft Steps.** She makes for a low wall above a worn flight of steps down toward the water — and here is where the scene pays out no matter what. To clear the wall one-handed she slings the oilcloth fold off her shoulder (or it's jolted loose as she goes over); it skids down onto the steps. **A pursuer can snatch it up** — that costs them their action this round, letting the Lead grow — **or mark where it fell and grab it after.** Keeping pace over the wall is **DC 15** (Athletics or Acrobatics).

::: gm
**The fold drops here, every time.** Whether the party catches her, loses her, or breaks off the chase, this is the moment the clue enters their hands or their eyeline. Everything after this is about the *person*, not the *payload*. (If the party would rather grab the fold than keep running, that's a perfectly good choice — let them, and let her go.)
:::

**3 · The Tanners' cut.** Into a reeking alley between the tanneries, where she upends a barrow of fleeces behind her. Pursuers vault or dodge it (Acrobatics or Dexterity), bull straight through (Athletics), or — if anyone knows the district — take the parallel cut and skip the mess (Survival/streetwise). **DC 15.** This is where she banks her lead; a fumble here costs a pursuer a round untangling from wet wool.

**4 · The culvert.** The lane ends at the Loft, where a culvert grate stands half-open at the waterline and a flat skiff sits poled and waiting in the reeds. She drops through the grate — too narrow for an armored adult — or into the skiff and out under a drain-arch into the dark of the under-streets.

::: gm
**This is her escape, and it should usually work.** Unless the party has *already* driven the Lead to zero **and** has a real way to follow — a flying or Small companion, a readied grapple, a *hold person* or *misty step*, water-walking — she is gone. If a player spends a genuine resource to cut the run short earlier (a spell, a clever readied action, Trina's help if she's nearby and willing), reward it generously: drop the Lead by two, or let them skip a stretch. The feeling you want is *the party did everything right and still met a professional on her home street.*
:::

### What she dropped — the fold

A flat oilcloth packet: a stub of chalk, a notched tally-stick, a half-written report-slip, and a folded instruction-card. Read it in layers, the way the party read the table:

- **What it is** (Investigation DC 10): surveillance. Someone has been logging *who visits the stationer's door and the green-coats' office.* The half-written slip even carries the party's own descriptions — and a line above them: *"watch-coat man — 1 hr — took nothing."* (That's the thief's recon. The party realizes the firm has had this street under glass for days.)
- **The drop** (Investigation DC 13, the instruction-card): she leaves her tallies at a dead-letter niche in the city — *behind the loose ashlar at the Coopers' Arms, on the Tannerway corner* — to be lifted each Sevenday. *"Keep nothing."* That's an immediate Loftwick thread: stake out the niche, trace the hand that empties it.
- **The route** (History or Investigation DC 16, the routing mark): the card bears a small clearing-glyph kin to the firm's tower-over-waves mark, and the line *"east on the Sevenday cart."* The reports don't stop in Loftwick — they ride to **Hardby.** Sparrow is the near end of a courier chain whose far end is the firm's Hardby hand.

Two pointers, guaranteed: a niche to watch *here*, and a road that runs *east.* The fold names a route and a firm-mark — never "E.," never the tube's strange craft, never a throne. It points at the **road**, not the **mastermind.**

### How it ends

However the run plays out, it lands somewhere clean:

- **She's gone, and you have the fold** *(the usual outcome).* A fair loss with a real prize — and the firm doesn't yet know its watcher is blown. The party leaves Wick Lane with the east-pointer in hand.
- **You catch her** *(only with real effort).* You have the fold *and* a terrified sixteen-year-old who can tell you only that *"a man left coppers at the drop — I never saw his face."* Now the scene's quiet test: what do they do with her? **Trina** will take her in without being asked — *"I have a back room, and a use for someone who knows the lanes."* **Caelith** will quietly relocate and pay her, the way he handles Reith. Roughing her up costs the party nothing on the sheet and something in the room, and a watching Trina remembers it. This is the conscience the chase exists to carry. *(Her capture blows the firm's surveillance — bring the squeeze of Movement Three a little forward.)*
- **You lose both** *(you broke off, or the dice were cruel).* The fold dropped at the Loft Steps regardless; that afternoon a fleece-seller returns it to the party — *"a runner dropped this; looked official"* — or Caelith's quiet contact does. The road still runs east.
- **You never engaged her at all.** She tails the party home, and *her* report is what tips the firm into the night's events. She turns up on Wick Lane again later for a second look. Nothing dead-ends.

::: gm
**A harder target, if your table wants teeth.** Swap Sparrow for the adult "leg" the firm actually runs its chain through — the runner Vesh sends out the back. Same four stretches, but the escape is a pre-positioned skiff rather than a child's culvert, and cornering him yields a description that matches the Hardby broker's circle (a stronger seed for the next chapter) but **no name**, under any roll — he's paid for silence the way professionals are. The trade is menace for conscience; run one or the other, not both.

**On the table (Foundry).** Use the *Loftwick — City* scene with token vision on; lay a light chain of street walls along Wick Lane → the Cloth Market → the Loft Steps → the Tanners' cut → the culvert, and mark the four stretches and the fold-drop. *(The map's own notes already flag it for exactly this.)*
:::

### Where this leads

Whatever else happened, the party walks off Wick Lane with the road drawn for them — the Hardby chop, the words "Hardby work," and "east on the Sevenday cart," all pointing the same way. Now the other neighborhood, where the conspiracy has a face and a family.

## Scene 4 · Tannerway

*A workaday street by the eastern caravan yard — leather, grain, and the smell of the road. Where the missing woman lived and worked.*

If Wick Lane is paper and pursuit, Tannerway is people. Nothing here is a trap and nothing here runs; this is where the party stops chasing a ledger and starts chasing a woman with a family and a clock on her. Three doors, and the stakes that turn the party's curiosity into urgency.

### 4a · The counting house — Doril Veth

Tamsin Moraven's counting house stands open and quietly grieving, run in her absence by her chief clerk, **Doril Veth**, who keeps her chair empty behind the standing desk and has been cataloguing her unfinished work because he expects her to want to resume it.

::: read
A narrow counting room: a standing desk at the front, a weighbench at the back, bound carbons in a wall niche, and behind the desk a good chair no one is sitting in. The man at the ledgers is perhaps fifty, careful, red-eyed in the way of someone who has decided not to cry at work. "You're here about Mistress Moraven," he says, before you've said so. "Good. Somebody should be." He turns the open book toward you. "She was *in the middle of something.* People in the middle of something do not simply walk off to a farm."
:::

Veth will show the party her last open ledger and help them read it. Her final three queries (Investigation DC 12) all reference **Cindren & Vhal Hardby** chops. Worse: her own carbon copies of two of those queries are gone (Investigation DC 15) — lifted from the bound book and replaced with blank impression-pages. *Someone reached into this room before the party did.* And if a character follows her trail of questions to its end (Investigation DC 16, or DC 14 with Trina reading alongside), the pattern she was building toward surfaces, and it is the worst thing in the chapter: the insurance claims on three South Province grain shipments — for storms, spoilage, seizure — were filed *before the losses happened.* By eleven days in one case; by three weeks in another.

> Caelith, when the party brings him the pattern: *"That is not theft. That is being paid to know what tomorrow looks like. Whoever ran this had a way to make tomorrow obey them."*

Read forward it's fraud; read backward it's something colder — the losses were arranged, the claims pre-positioned. Prediction, profit, erasure. Tamsin saw it. Then Tamsin vanished.

### 4b · The schoolmaster's house — Vellin Moraven

A short walk away, Tamsin's husband keeps their small house. **Vellin Moraven** is a quiet schoolmaster who has aged a year in eleven days, and he carries his fear so gently that it's worse to watch than weeping.

::: read
The schoolmaster receives you in a front parlor that is too tidy, the tidiness of a man who has nothing to do with his hands. He offers tea he forgets to pour. When you ask about his wife he is quiet a moment, then takes a slip of paper from the desk — already worn at the folds, as if it's been opened many times — and lays it flat for you to read. One line, in a firm woman's hand: *"If I am not back by Sevenday, take Ailen out of Hardby."* "Ailen is our daughter," he says. "She's studying with a sculptor. In Hardby." He does not finish the thought. He hasn't been able to write the letter.
:::

The note does two things: it confirms Tamsin knew she was in danger and going anyway, and it puts a name and a face in **Hardby** — their daughter Ailen, unprotected, in the city the whole chapter points toward. Gently suggesting Vellin send for her tonight is the right move (Insight DC 12 tells the party as much). If Trina is with the party she'll write to Ailen herself, by her own courier — a poet she sponsors, leaving for Hardby that very night — and the letter may pay off when the party reaches Hardby in the next chapter.

### 4c · The watch house — Sergeant Halir Anwic

If the party calls at the watch house, they find **Sergeant Halir Anwic**, honest, overworked, and quietly ashamed of how thin his report on Moraven is.

::: read
The sergeant doesn't make excuses, which is somehow worse. "No body, no struggle, no weight behind the name," he says, reciting the categories that let a file go cold. "So I marked it open and unescalated and I moved on to the ones that were shouting at me." He rubs his face. "I didn't look hard. I should have. Tell me you're looking, and I'll look with you."
:::

Anwic will reopen the file at Caelith's word (not before — he's a careful man and it isn't his to reopen on a stranger's say-so), and from then on he's a Watch ally worth having. If the chapter's pressure later forces him off the case, he finds the party privately first and presses his notes into their hands as a parting gift.

### The verb that matters

Somewhere in working Tannerway, the party should land on the line that makes the whole travel choice hurt. Tamsin is *MORAVEN delayed* — not *settled.* Hold Caelith to the distinction:

> *"I do not say it as a comfort. I say it because the verb is not 'settled.' She is somewhere they have not yet decided to close her account. They will, eventually, decide. Whether they decide before you reach her depends on how you travel."*

Alive, and on a clock. That is the stake the party carries to the final scene.

### Where Movement Two leads

When the party pools what both neighborhoods gave them, don't have Caelith announce the destination — lay the threads side by side and let *them* draw the line. Gilst's bank instrument is a **Hardby** chop. Vesh said *Hardby* work. The chase fold says *east on the Sevenday cart.* Tamsin's last queries were *Hardby* queries. Ailen is in *Hardby.* And Veska Maelan — the trade-clearing notary Caelith named on the night they took the commission — is in *Hardby*, and can read these chops better than anyone outside the firm. Six threads, one city. The party will see it. That recognition *is* the motion this chapter was built to produce.

## Interlude · The Mentor

Woven through the whole chapter, not a scene of its own, is the slow, quiet thing between **Trina Alvere** and the party's warlock, **Alicia.** It is the soft counterweight to all this administrative cold, and you should ration it — *one beat per session, no more* — so each lands with weight. Trina recognizes Alicia's pact-bond on sight; she will not name her own patron, or Alicia's, in any room, and **you must not name Trina's patron either,** under any roll. (See *Things you never name.*) Use these as the moments arise:

- **The first recognition** — most likely at the morning table, while the rest of the party is bent over the ledger fragment. Trina takes the chair beside Alicia and says, softly: *"We share a quiet teacher. I will not name her in this room. I will not name yours either. I only want you to know I am here, and that I am proud of you for being here."* If Alicia takes her offered hand, a small thing passes between them, and Alicia knows — without testing — that Trina is, somehow, family.
- **The gift of the moth scarf** — when Alicia seems despondent (after Moll's pressure, say, or when Gilst's murder lands). Trina presses a folded silk scarf embroidered with moths into her hand: *"Wear it under your collar, and on the day you most need a flicker of light, hold it closed and speak my given name."* (Mechanically: one charge of *faerie fire*, no slot or components or concentration, the moths finding whichever enemy Alicia means; it renews after a long rest in Trina's presence, and is a one-shot once the party leaves the city.)
- **After Moll** — Trina catches Alicia in the corridor: *"He is not powerful. He is loud. The two are not the same. Do you know the difference?"* If Alicia answers anything thoughtful, Trina nods once, says *"Good,"* and moves on.
- **After the murder lands** — Trina shows Alicia a vellum-bound book on a low library shelf: two centuries of poems by Loftwick clerks, none famous, all mourned by someone who paid for the binding. *"There are about thirty names in here. There will be one more by next year. The work is not stopping a clerk from dying. The work is making sure the next one knows why we keep doing the work."*

If a fight ever puts Alicia down while Trina is present, Trina spends her reaction to *misty step* to her and her action to stabilize — she will not trade blows, only pull the girl out of the line and concede. She is a friend. That is all the party learns of her this chapter, and it is enough.

# Movement Three — The Squeeze and the Road

Loftwick stops being a safe place to work. The conspiracy can't reach the party's *minds*, so it reaches for the things around them: a genial official with a bribe, and a thief in the night. Then Caelith lays the road on the table.

## Scene 5 · The Friendly Wall — Arthen Moll

Trigger this once the party has named at least two of the three (Cindren & Vhal, Gilst, Moraven) and hasn't yet committed to a way east — the afternoon of the evidence review, or the next morning if they were out late. **Arthen Moll**, Deputy Voice of Civic Reconciliation, is the friendliest obstacle they'll meet in Loftwick, and the most instructive: he is not corrupt, and he is completely in the conspiracy's way of wanting.

He arrives by appointment, two clerks and their portfolios in tow, a card sent up ahead. Caelith quietly suggests receiving him in the dense, unglamorous Library rather than the Reception Parlor — harder for a man like Moll to perform in. Trina, if she's about, slips in behind him and takes the chair at the window, and says nothing unless spoken to.

::: read
Moll smiles when he sees you — the same warm smile he gave the footman, the same one he'll give the cook on his way out. He doesn't sit until you do. "Friends of the Common Defense," he says, with feeling. "What you have done for the Yeomanry will be remembered for at least a full council session. I'll not flatter you with more than that. I've come on a small errand, which I think you'll find a sensible one.

"The matter you're turning over — the bonded firms, the unfortunate clerk, the missing factor on Tannerway — is, as you've surely seen, a matter of account-keeping. The Yeomanry has institutions for account-keeping. The Treasury, the Audit Hall, the Bureau. Slow, yes. But ours, and older than any of us. The Office of Civic Reconciliation would like to say, formally and with gratitude, that you have done *enough.* We'd like you to take a small honorarium — twelve hundred gold each, by Yeomanry draft, not Council coin — and a quiet departure to wherever you're next needed. You'll be named in the durable record as the friends who lit the candle. We will, with our slower hands, carry it to its lamp."

He looks pleasantly at each of you in turn. "If you accept, we can have the drafts here by sundown. If you don't — and I say this because I respect you — we will not block your inquiry; the office hasn't the power, and wouldn't use it if it had. We'll only ask you to weigh that *visible* inquiry by friends-of-the-Yeomanry into an Aerdi-licensed firm has a cost to our trade. The eastern banks watch. They'll read your visibility as policy, and we'll pay for it in basis points for two quarters. That's all. May I leave the drafts, in case you change your minds tonight?"
:::

What's actually on the table: the bribe is **real** (1,200 gp each, clean money from a real fund), and the threat is institutional, not violent — Moll can't stop the party, but he *can* file a note with the Council that costs Caelith political capital, and he will. He believes every word (Insight DC 12 confirms his sincerity; DC 15 catches that the line about "the eastern banks watch" isn't his own — he's repeating someone else's argument; DC 18 recognizes the whole speech as a script he's run three times this season). He is the wall the soft-pressure faction puts up before things get loud — and the party's own visible legwork is what summoned him. If the chase ran loud and public, feel free to bring him a day early on the strength of it.

::: gm
**The decision and its consequences:**

- **Decline** *(the path the chapter expects).* Moll smiles, gathers his drafts, and leaves the door open behind him. Caelith, told of the refusal, is visibly relieved and doesn't say so. **Advance the Pressure Clock one segment** — and within a day, Cindren & Vhal (via Vesh's earlier message) confirm the party won't be administratively contained, and **escalate.** Merev Sarth comes the following night (Scene 6).
- **Defer** *("we'll think about it").* He leaves the drafts and returns next morning. Advance the clock anyway; Sarth comes the night the party finally decides.
- **Accept.** Let them. Run a session of downtime — spend the honoraria, train, craft. *Then,* three weeks on, Caelith arrives with the bill the party didn't pay: Tamsin's body has surfaced in the Sheldomar, Vellin and Ailen are gone, Pollow is dead, and Caelith is being recalled. The conspiracy used the time the party wouldn't. Most tables re-engage hard, now owing both Caelith and themselves. *(It's a supported path, not a punishment — but the disappointment in the room teaches the lesson better than any lecture.)*

If the party asks Trina afterward what she made of him, she sets her cup down: *"He believes what he says. That is the worst kind of obstacle. You cannot persuade him. You can only outwork him."*
:::

## Scene 6 · The Night Visitor — Merev Sarth

The night after the party rebuffs Moll, the firm tries the other kind of pressure. **Merev Sarth** — a professional thief, the "watchman" who searched Gilst's rooms — comes for the evidence in the cellar strongroom. Run this as tense, not lethal: she is on a *retrieval* contract, paid extra **not** to leave bodies, and she runs the instant the job goes wrong.

Her plan is clean. She comes over the courtyard wall in a real courier's tabard with a forged Audit Hall inspection writ, talks or slips past the gate guard, goes down the spiral stair to the strongroom, picks its double lock (she has a wax cast of the second tumbler, taken by the firm's clerk during a "Yeomanry inspection"), empties the chest into her wallet, and leaves not the way she came but through the old service tunnel in the cellar's east wall. What she burns, if she's cornered, she burns rather than leave behind.

::: gm
**What Sarth doesn't know — and what makes this a trap, not a heist:**

- The chest holds **decoys.** Caelith and Trina expected this the moment Vesh's runner left Wick Lane; the real evidence has been at Trina's townhouse since sundown.
- The old service tunnel — her clever exit — has been watched for three days. A steady Yeomanry corporal, **Brell Kostan**, has been posted at its far end since morning.
- There's a soft **alarm rune** on the inside of the strongroom door (Caelith's own work; a character inside can spot it for what it is at Arcana DC 14). It chimes in the upper hall and Caelith's study.

**How it plays.** If anyone in the party is awake and about (a night-owl studying in the library, someone who heard a sound), they can intercept her anywhere on her route — and her priority is *exit,* never victory: smoke pellet, Disengage, run for the tunnel. If the party is asleep, the rune wakes Caelith, and Caelith and Trina go down with lanterns to find her at the door or mid-transfer; Caelith doesn't draw a blade, he just tells her, evenly, to leave the wallet and take the tunnel as she planned, *"and the corporal at the other end will explain what happens next."* Recognizing two far more dangerous people than her client warned her of, she drops it and runs — into Kostan.

**If it comes to a fight:** she's quick and slippery (CR 4 — AC 15, HP 65, Sneak Attack, Uncanny Dodge, Evasion), but the strongroom is a cramped 10-by-12 box and she's there to *leave,* not to win. She uses smoke to blind the door, not the room. **At 30 HP or below she yields**; offered the chance to surrender she takes it. Run her as a kill-the-party encounter and you've misread the scene.
:::

What the night is worth: the party keeps every real piece of evidence (it was never in the chest), and they gain a captured thief, a forged Audit Hall seal, and — once Sarth talks — a name. Held lawfully a couple of days and offered Caelith's standing deal (a Yeomanry passport and a hundred gold for one future testimony; Persuasion DC 14 and she takes it, being a contractor and not a fanatic), she gives up that her paymaster was a Hardby intermediary she knows only as **"Tarsh"** — a thin man in his fifties with a smoker's voice — and that she was paid in laundered drafts drawn on banks that *aren't* Cindren & Vhal. She will die before naming the broker who connects her to work; don't push it. *(Advance the Pressure Clock one segment regardless of how the night ends — the firm now knows neither bribe nor burglary moved the party, and the next push will be worse.)*

::: gm
Two threads quietly tighten here. The **forged Audit Hall seal** on Sarth's writ matches a counterfeit stamp Caelith has been hunting in his own office for nine months — the leak, again, brushed and not named. And if the party staked out the **drop-niche** from the chase (Scene 3d), let it pay off now: they recognize the courier's mark, or anticipate the retrieval, because the watcher's reports and Sarth's contract ran through the same hand. The two squeezes were always one hand. Let that land.
:::

## Scene 7 · Three Roads East

By the third morning the evidence is still on the long table, the city is going about its day outside, and Caelith judges the party ready to choose how they leave. He sets the copied countermark beside the clipped coin.

::: read
"Cindren and Vhal," Caelith says. "Bonded factors. Respectable enough for clean rooms. Useful enough for dirty ones." He pins the eastern coast to a small map between the squares of linen. "Their house is in Rel Astra, the Old Harbor Ward. The senior partners answer to no one in this kingdom or the next, in any way the law would let us reach them." Outside, bells ring. "I do not know whether Haskur Vandrell is there. But someone there was ready for him." He looks at each of you in turn. "And if we have learned this much by morning — then by nightfall they will know we learned it."
:::

Then he calls the party up to the cold, disused Old Council Chamber, where Trina is already seated among the ring of carved chairs, and lays three leather folios on the round table. Three ways east, and — as he says it himself, a finger on each in turn — **"I can give you authority, cover, or distance. I cannot give you all three."**

- **The Official Delegation** *(authority).* The party travels as accredited Yeomanry representatives, with credentials, a junior councilman to lend his name, clerks, and a guard escort. It is **slow** — the speed of paperwork, with a formal stop and a written report at every customs post — and **visible**: the firm will know they're coming and exactly when, with all the time in the world to sanitize records and move witnesses. But Aerdi authorities can't touch accredited foreign inquirers, the firm is bound by law to receive them, and any witness they recover can travel home under diplomatic cover. *(Filing the credentials advances the Pressure Clock two segments at once, and one more per week of travel — the loudest road.)*
- **Commercial Cover** *(cover — Caelith's quiet recommendation).* The party travels as a small wool-buying delegation assessing eastern grain markets, with real samples, real letters of introduction, and Trina's letter to the poet **Castrian Vell**, who keeps an ear in four Hardby salons. **Faster** than the delegation, and it buys *plausible access* — wool merchants visit counting houses for credit all the time — but it demands **discipline**: anyone who breaks character risks the group, and a paladin in plate or a gnome bard known to the Hardby tavern circuit thins the cover fast. *(It doesn't advance the clock at departure; it advances only if the cover seriously breaks.)*
- **Quiet Pursuit** *(distance).* Fresh horses, light packs, false papers, no official capacity. **Fastest by a wide margin**, and the only road that *surprises* the firm — they won't know the party is moving until it arrives in Hardby. But there's no net: caught east of Hardby with false papers, the party are just liars to the local watch, and Caelith says it plainly — *"If you are taken, I cannot reach you."* *(It doesn't advance the clock at departure either, unless the party is recognized in Hardby and can't slip away.)*

Caelith will hear a fourth idea if the party has one — sea passage from Saltmarsh (slower, but it bypasses Hardby entirely), their own teleport magic (he won't provide one and is wary of the Aerdi reading magical arrivals) — and he'll advise against splitting up: *"You are stronger than my agents because you are together."* Trina, asked, says nothing about the first or third roads; of the second she says only, *"Castrian's house is mine in everything but the address. He will know you are mine. Do not embarrass him."*

::: gm
**Before the folios hit the table, run your gate check** — the five truths from the front of this chapter. The party should leave Loftwick certain that Haskur was kept alive on purpose, that he was a node in a network, that the firm's living hand is in Hardby, that Loftwick won't let them finish here, and that the road runs east and must be taken now. If any of the five is thin, the *Quick reference* tells you which late line shores it up. Then let them choose their road — and make sure they feel the weight of it, because **this choice shapes the next chapter.** Speed may save Tamsin; visibility may get her "settled." (The exact consequences of each road are in the *Hand-off to the next chapter* table at the back.)
:::

When they ride out, read them onto the road:

::: read
Loftwick is at its quietest in the hour you leave. The Civic Way is broad and pale; the lamps along the Audit Hall still burn though the sky has gone the color of new milk. At the Little Palace gate the footmen raise their lanterns once and lower them — the old Yeomanry sign for safe travel.

Caelith stands at the gate. He embraces no one. He steps to the lead rider and offers a small flat brass token — the quill above the closed gate. "If you find the man who underwrote him, write to me at this house, with this on the seal. Three days from now I will be in this office, or its successor. The token will reach me either way."

Trina is at his shoulder. She has already said what she needed to say, last night, in private, to the last of you she walked with. The silver leaf at her collar catches the early light. She lifts a hand, and lowers it.

The road bends out of the Civic Way onto the Wool Road, and by the time the walls drop behind you the lamps of the Audit Hall have gone out. You ride an hour before anyone speaks, and when someone finally does, you find that none of you has yet asked the question you all carry: *what was waiting for him in Rel Astra — and is it waiting still.*
:::

Hardby is six days east if they press the horses. Rel Astra is eleven on the map, and, on Caelith's quiet reckoning, closer than that — he hasn't said by how much. The next chapter opens at the gates of Hardby, whichever road they took.

# Quick Reference

*Everything in this chapter, condensed for the table. Read the chapter once; run from these pages.*

## The five truths, and how to guarantee them

Tick these as they land. Each is carried by several clues, so a missed roll never costs the party a truth — but if you reach Scene 7 and one is thin, use the backstop line.

| The party must believe… | Carried by | If it's thin at the gate, use… |
|---|---|---|
| **1.** Haskur was kept alive on purpose. | Caelith's speech (two prior "deaths"); the burned focus; the pre-dated letter of credit. | Caelith: *"Twice before, someone caught him before the ground did. That is not luck. That is a budget."* |
| **2.** He's a node in a network, not a lone man. | The three-hand ledger fragment; Gilst's four-year retainer; the inside-job forgery. | Caelith: *"He has had four names that I can prove and a face for none of them. Men like that are issued, not born."* |
| **3.** Cindren & Vhal are central; the hand is in Hardby. | The seal + countermark; Gilst's Hardby bank instrument; Vesh's "Hardby work"; Tamsin's Hardby queries; the chase fold. | Caelith, tapping the bank instrument: *"One chop. On his money and on the dead man's. Hardby drew them both."* |
| **4.** Loftwick won't let them finish here. | Moll's bribe; Sarth's night; the Pressure Clock's escalations; the watcher. | Let the next clock segment fire — its telegraph *is* the proof. |
| **5.** The road runs east, and now. | The six converging threads; Tamsin "delayed," not "settled"; Caelith's conclusion. | Trina: *"The thing you are looking for is not in Loftwick. I will push where Caelith will not."* |

## The Pressure Clock in full

Put six segments on a notecard where the players can see them. **Advance it when the conspiracy can *see* the party act** (showing their hand at the firm; rattling Pollow; a public spectacle; the Sarth night) and when a whole day passes with nothing done. **Don't** advance it for quiet, honest work. Announce the trigger each time, and read the telegraph aloud — every tick is a gift of momentum, not a punishment.

| Seg. | What the party sees that morning | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| **1 — Tampering** | Caelith: an item in the chest sits one inch from where he set it. *"Someone was looking. Not for what we expected."* | The cellar isn't safe; the firm has a hand inside. |
| **2 — Warning** | Trina lays a note on the table: someone has told her to stop hosting the party. *"The neighbor is mistaken."* She doesn't budge. | The firm knows who their hosts are. It's personal now. |
| **3 — Rumor** | A broadsheet at midday asks what the Audit Hall keeps in its little palace, and who is *foreign* in the question. By tea-time the party is recognizable in the street. | Visibility now has a price (and summons Moll). |
| **4 — Witness loss** | Cut the thread that hurts most: Pollow found dead, or Anwic reassigned, or Gilst's rooms cleaned out before the party returns. | Dawdling has a cost. Urgency, purchased the hard way. |
| **5 — The motion** | Elsbet hands Caelith a Council card. He reads it standing. *"The motion is filed."* The Audit Hall withdraws its quiet support; the party has 48 hours. | A fuse. It pushes them to the gate. |
| **6 — The door shuts** | GM's choice, used only if the clock truly burns out: Tamsin confirmed dead, or Caelith recalled, or Vellin taken. | The next chapter begins under worse conditions. |

**If the clock runs hot** (segment 5 with threads still open): let Caelith burn a favor to buy 24 hours — *"I have paid for one more morning. Use it."* — or cut straight to Scene 7; the road *is* the pressure release. **If it runs cold** (still at 1 going into the last session): bring Moll back with a clerk and a quiet threat, have Trina urge departure, or have Caelith pin the brass token to Rel Astra on the map and say the quiet part: *"At Loftwick speed, we lose Tamsin, and we lose Ailen. I would rather not."*

## If they wander — getting them back on course

The party can't break this chapter, only slow it. When they drift, reach for the gentlest tool that works and climb only as needed:

First, **let a thread tug.** Trina mentions, in passing, that Ailen is alone in Hardby. The human stake re-aims a drifting party better than any map.

If that's not enough, **let the clock tick** for the wasted day and read its telegraph — each segment is a fresh event that hands the party something to do.

If they're still adrift, **let an ally push.** Caelith pins the map; Trina says the thing Caelith won't. They have standing to be blunt; use it.

And if the party has truly stalled, **collapse a thread they were dawdling on** — fire a witness-loss segment. Losing something re-prices everything.

For the specific worries: if they **skip Gilst's rooms,** the Hardby chop still reaches them through Vesh's "Hardby work" and the chase fold, and Caelith's contact can recover the bank instrument before the room is cleaned. If they **skip Tannerway,** Trina carries Ailen and the Sevenday note to them unasked, and Anwic can bring Tamsin's file to Caelith. If they **never trigger the chase,** the watcher tails them home and tips the firm — folding into Moll and Sarth — and the fold's pointer is redundant with five other threads. If they **catch the watcher instantly,** you lose nothing: the fold is the prize, and now there's a frightened girl and a choice. If they **take Moll's bribe,** run downtime, then send Caelith with the bill (see Scene 5). If they **try to ride straight past Hardby to Rel Astra,** let them — but price it in Caelith's voice first: no Tamsin, no Tarsh, no Veska Maelan, no Castrian Vell, and a far harder next chapter. If they **try to storm the firm's seniors now,** the seniors are in Rel Astra behind a wall the Loftwick satellite genuinely cannot open — that's real, in-fiction, not your hand on the scale. And if the party **insists on splitting** across both neighborhoods, call them to a working lunch at the Little Palace to pool what they found; the half-day costs a clock segment, and the table stays together.

## Things you never name

The chapter changes *how the party moves*, never *what stays secret*. Hold these exactly:

- **"E." is never identified** — not a name, not a people, not a faction. The party collects the initial and leaves with the question open. *(There is an answer. It is years away from this table.)*
- **The lacquered tube is "elven, but peculiar" — never anything more.** Under any roll, the peculiarity *is* the answer. The strangest word the campaign is protecting does not get spoken here, and *legend lore* yields only a fragment about a people who "walked downward and were forgotten."
- **HARBOR BEFORE THRONE stays a slogan of intent** — no throne, no harbor, no succession named yet.
- **The leak in Caelith's office is implied, never pinned.** The inside forgery, the tampering, and Sarth's forged seal all point inward — and Caelith will not name a clerk he can't prove. Neither do you.
- **Trina's patron is never named,** not even to Alicia. Past her wall lies only: *"I share a teacher. I have not finished my lessons."*
- **The chase fold points at the road, not the mastermind** — a route and a firm-mark, never "E." or the tube's craft.

If you slip, don't panic-retcon. *"That's what's on the page. There's no other answer in this room."* The campaign survives one slip.

## Hand-off to the next chapter

The road the party chose in Scene 7 sets the conditions the next chapter opens under. Price the choice; don't editorialize it — none of these is a failure.

| Road taken | Time the firm has had | Tamsin Moraven, in Hardby | Their Hardby contacts |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Official Delegation** (authority) | ~2 weeks | **Confirmed dead** — a body recovered days before they arrive. | Witnesses scattered; the broker moved upcountry; a paper trail only. |
| **Commercial Cover** (cover) | ~1 week | **Alive, moved** to a coastal estate two days east — recoverable with effort. | Veska Maelan and Castrian Vell both reachable; the broker confrontable. |
| **Quiet Pursuit** (distance) | ~3 days | **Alive, still in Hardby** — held in a warehouse, a live rescue. | Every contact unprepared and within reach; the broker easiest of all. |

The chase fold pays off here too: *"east on the Sevenday cart"* is the near end of a courier chain the party meets again in Hardby. When they recognize the mark, let it be a small earned *"we've seen this before"* — a thread tied off, not a shortcut taken.

## The cast, at a glance

**Allies.** *Caelith Dunivar* — the Hand of the Duke; answers facts, refuses guesses, will not name an unproven leak. *Trina Alvere* — his advisor, Alicia's quiet mentor; warm, observant, immovable; her patron is never named. *Elsbet Vael* — the steward; knows the household, discreet about everything that matters.

**Witnesses.** *Doman Reith* — Gilst's frightened landlord. *Doril Veth* — Tamsin's grieving chief clerk. *Vellin Moraven* — her husband; the Sevenday note. *Sgt Halir Anwic* — the watchman who let her file cool, looking to make it right.
